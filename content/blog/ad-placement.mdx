# Why I Dropped AdSense for a Premium Ad Partner

When I first launched PolishedDex, ads weren't even on my radar. The site was a side project — something I built because I wanted better docs for the ROM hacks I was playing. No revenue model, no plan. Just a thing I made.

Then it grew. A lot. Hundreds of thousands of monthly users showing up to look up stats, evolutions, item locations. And suddenly I was paying real money to keep it running. So I did what everyone does first — I added AdSense.

It was fine. For a while.

---

## Where AdSense Started Falling Apart

As traffic grew, AdSense went from "fine" to actively working against me:

- **Low CPMs.** AdSense's open auction doesn't compete well against premium demand. For a niche gaming audience with high engagement, I was being massively underpaid.
- **No layout control.** Auto ads would inject units in random spots — between table rows, inside navigation, mid-sentence. Horrible for a site where people need to scan data quickly.
- **CLS nightmares.** Ads loading late would shove content around. On a database site where people are comparing stats side-by-side, layout shift is unacceptable.
- **One-size-fits-all.** No ability to treat a dense stat page differently from a long-form guide page.

I spent more time fighting AdSense's auto-placement than actually improving the site. And I started to realize — this wasn't a configuration problem. I'd outgrown it.

---

## Making the Switch

I'd been putting off looking into alternatives because AdSense was "good enough" and switching felt like a big commitment. But once I started researching managed ad partners — the kind that require a traffic threshold to join — I realized how much I'd been leaving on the table.

The difference once I actually made the switch:

- **Header bidding with premium demand.** Multiple ad exchanges compete in real-time instead of Google's single auction. CPMs jumped immediately.
- **Full placement control.** I decide exactly where units go in the DOM. No auto-injection, no surprises.
- **Lazy loading built in.** Ads below the fold don't load until they're actually approaching the viewport. Better performance, better viewability scores, better revenue.
- **Dedicated support.** Someone who actually looks at my site and suggests layout optimizations specific to reference content.

The tradeoff is less automation — I have to be intentional about where ads go. But that's actually the point.

---

## Learning to Think About Placement

With full control came a new problem: I actually had to decide where ads go. No more "let Google figure it out."

People come to PolishedDex with a specific question: "What level does this evolve?" or "Where do I find this item?" Every pixel between them and that answer is friction. So I had to figure out a philosophy pretty quickly.

So the rule is simple: **ads never interrupt the primary lookup flow.**

Where they do go:

1. **Below the main data block** — you found your answer, now there's an ad before related content
2. **Between sections on long pages** — natural content breaks where you'd pause anyway
3. **Sidebar on wide viewports** — alongside content, not above it
4. **After engagement is confirmed** — never on initial page load above the fold

What I don't do: interstitials, sticky footers that cover content, auto-playing video, or anything that makes the site feel hostile.

---

## Was It Worth It?

Honestly? I wish I'd done it sooner. The switch paid for itself almost immediately:

- Revenue per session more than doubled compared to AdSense
- Core Web Vitals improved because I control when and where ads render
- Bounce rate didn't change — meaning the ads aren't driving people away
- I actually get paid what the traffic is worth instead of whatever Google's auction decides

The biggest lesson from this whole journey: AdSense optimizes for Google's revenue, not yours. Once you have the traffic to qualify for a premium partner, there's almost no reason to stay.

---

## What I'd Tell Past Me

If you're running a content site with real traffic and you're still on AdSense by default — look into alternatives. The threshold to join a managed partner is usually 50-100k monthly pageviews. Once you're past that, you're probably leaving 2-3x revenue on the table.

I put it off for months because it felt like a big deal. It wasn't. The integration took a day, the results were immediate, and I got back time I was spending fighting auto-placement to spend on actually building features.

And if your site is a reference tool where people need to find information fast — placement discipline matters more than fill rate. Users don't mind ads. They mind ads that get between them and the answer they came for.
